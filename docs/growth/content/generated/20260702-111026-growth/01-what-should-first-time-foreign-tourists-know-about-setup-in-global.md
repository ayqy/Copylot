## What First-Time Foreign Tourists Need to Know About Setup in Global

### 1. The Real Problem: Staying Connected and Mobile
When you travel abroad for the first time, you face three immediate setup challenges: getting a working phone number, accessing money, and using public transit. Without these, you can't book rides, pay for meals, or navigate. This guide covers what actually works.

### 2. Step-by-Step Setup Decisions
#### SIM Card or eSIM?
- **Physical SIM**: Buy at airport kiosks. Works in any unlocked phone. Costs $10-$30 for a week. **Risk**: Losing the tiny card.
- **eSIM**: Activate online before departure. Requires an eSIM-compatible phone (iPhone XS or later, most recent Androids). Costs $5-$15. **Risk**: Phone must be unlocked; carrier may not support.
- **Recommendation**: Use eSIM if your phone supports it; otherwise, buy a physical SIM at a reputable airport store (avoid random street vendors).

#### Banking & Payments
- Notify your home bank of travel dates to avoid card blockage.
- Carry a backup: a second credit card or prepaid travel card like Wise or Revolut.
- Keep some local cash: many small shops and taxis don't accept cards.
- **Failure scenario**: If both cards are blocked, use Wise or PayPal via a friend's account. Preload your card before departure.

#### Public Transit & Navigation
- Download offline maps (Google Maps or Maps.me) before you arrive.
- Buy a transit card at the airport or use contactless credit cards (check if your card supports tap-to-pay).
- **Common mistake**: Assuming all transit cards work system-wide—some are city-only (e.g., Tokyo's Suica vs. national IC cards).

### 3. Common Misconceptions & Boundaries
- **"WiFi everywhere" is false**: Free WiFi is spotty. You need a mobile data plan for maps, translation, and booking.
- **"My roaming plan covers everything"**: Many domestic plans charge exorbitant fees or throttle data after 500 MB. Check before you leave.
- **"I can figure it out when I arrive"**: Airport queues for SIM cards can be 2+ hours. Book or order online in advance.
- **"All ATMs are safe"**: Avoid standalone ATMs on the street; use bank-affiliated machines inside branches.

### 4. Fallback Plan When Things Fail
- **SIM failure**: Download offline apps before departure (Google Translate, Maps, an offline city guide). Use free WiFi in malls or libraries.
- **Card declined**: Have emergency cash (e.g., $200 in local currency). Western Union can wire money in minutes.
- **Transit card not accepted**: Ride-hailing apps like Uber or Grab often accept credit cards and work without a local SIM.
