## What Is Pricing and Why It Matters

Pricing is the mechanism you use to capture value from your product. It's not a one-time decision; it's a strategic lever that affects acquisition, retention, and revenue. For AI and software products, the wrong pricing can kill growth before you even get started. This guide walks you through a practical, step-by-step approach to set your pricing and avoid costly mistakes.

## Step 1: Define Your Value Metric

Your value metric is what you charge for. It must correlate with the value your customer receives. Common metrics:
- **Usage**: API calls, tokens, storage (good for variable consumption)
- **Seats**: per user (good for collaboration tools)
- **Tiers**: flat fee for feature sets (good for segmenting different needs)

Choose one metric that scales naturally with customer success. If you sell an AI agent, charging per task completed aligns better than per user.

## Step 2: Choose a Pricing Model

### Usage-Based Pricing
Charge for what customers consume. Examples: OpenAI per token, AWS per compute hour. Works well for products with variable usage patterns. But customers dislike unpredictable bills.

### Tiered Pricing
Fixed packages (e.g., Basic $10, Pro $50, Enterprise $200). Simple to understand and sell. Risk: customers may outgrow your top tier or feel forced into unnecessary features.

### Hybrid Model
Combine a base fee with usage add-ons. Example: $20/month for 1,000 credits, then $0.01 per extra credit. Helps balance predictability and scalability.

## Step 3: Test and Validate

Don't guess. Use these methods:
- **Surveys**: Ask potential customers what they'd pay. Be specific: "Would you pay $30/month for X? Why or why not?"
- **A/B Testing**: Show different prices to small segments. Measure conversion and retention.
- **Trial-to-Paid**: Let users experience the product first, then offer pricing at the end of trial. See what converts.

## Common Misconceptions and Pitfalls

### 1. Underpricing to Get Customers
**Problem**: Low price attracts bargain hunters who churn fast. You also signal low value.
**Fix**: Start higher than you think. You can always lower prices later, but raising them is hard.

### 2. Ignoring Churn
**Problem**: If your pricing doesn't align with retention (e.g., annual contracts with no win-back plan), you lose revenue.
**Fix**: Monitor churn per pricing tier. Adjust early.

### 3. One Size Fits All
**Problem**: Single price fails to capture different customer segments.
**Fix**: Create at least two tiers: a low-cost entry and a premium option. Research shows this increases average revenue per user.

## What If Your Pricing Fails?

Have a backup plan:
- **Limited-time discount**: Temporarily lower price to test elasticity. E.g., offer 30% off for first 3 months.
- **Freemium model**: Free tier with limited features to drive adoption, then upsell.
- **Pivot to value metric**: If churn is high, you may be charging on the wrong metric. Switch from per-user to per-usage, or vice versa.
